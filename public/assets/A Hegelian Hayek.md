# Is Hegel Really Incompatible with Hayek?

Hayek, my main influence in economics, is usually seen as the polar opposite of Hegel, my main influence in philosophy. On the surface, the two are irreconcilable—especially when you throw in the French extensions of Hegel, and you have a recipe for theoretical indigestion. Yet I want to argue that this incompatibility is largely performative. If we strip away Hayek's more dogmatic political polemics and the naive post-Kantian reading of Hegel, there could be a surprising convergence.

For individual freedom, Hayek starts with the individual, with their dispersed, tacit knowledge. But this is a fiction—in the most productive sense of the word. As Hegel noted in the Philosophy of Right, the individual who believes they are acting in pure self-interest is already embedded in a web of social institutions. There is no atomic self making choices in a vacuum. Hegel's insight, which we can radicalize here, is that true freedom is not the absence of constraint but the presence of universal conditions. An individual is not free if they are constraint to basic necessities to survive. Only with that already been taken care of, then one can pursue self-actualization. Hayek argued that freedom is the ability to act on one's own knowledge. But what happens when that "ability" is structurally blocked by poverty, lack of information, or precarity? This is not an argument for leftist-style central planning. It is an argument for what Hegel called the "State" as the mediator of universality. In the Hegelian sense, the State is not an external enemy of the market; it is the market's own logic pushed to its conclusion. In fact, Hayek himself said that "in an advanced society government ought to use its power of raising funds by taxation to provide a number of services which for various reasons cannot be provided, or cannot be provided adequately, by the market." The question is not whether the State should intervene, but how.

A common misconception—or the simple reading of Hegel—is that the Absolute Spirit functions as an all-knowing totality, something like God in the traditional sense. But from post-structuralist philosophers like Derrida and Lacan, we know that there is no big Other. And this insight allows us to reinterpret Hegel in a much more radical, anti-foundationalist way. Interestingly, this is also precisely Hayek's point. Hayek's entire critique of central planning rests on the fact that knowledge is always partial, local, incomplete. No central agency can possess the totality required to direct an economy from above. In this sense, Hayek and the Hegel share a fundamental premise: there is no subject supposed to know. One could argue, however, that Hayek still retains a metaphysical residue. Not the "invisible hand" as a secular deity—Hayek himself was too sophisticated for that—but rather a faith in the self-correcting tendency of spontaneous order. The system, for Hayek, carries within itself the capacity to rectify its own errors, provided the state does not interfere. This is a milder but still significant metaphysics: the belief that the logic of the system, when allowed to operate freely, tends toward coherence rather than crisis. The Hegelian, by contrast, expects crisis as internal to the system's logic, not a deviation from it.

If we accept this systemic view, then crisis should be seen as a symptom rather than the root of the problem. Crisis reveals that the system has reached a point of internal contradiction. The proper response is not to deny the contradiction, but to let it work itself out toward localized adjustments that prevent systemic accumulation of risk. After 2008, Wall Street and financial leaders became too afraid to embrace the natural losses. If we were truly allowing loss and failure, we would ideally see small, regional crises rather than global meltdowns, as players would constantly speculate, adjust, and absorb shocks at a manageable scale. But due to state interventions that protect incumbents and political lobbying that distorts the rules, these necessary adjustments are postponed and delayed. This is like taking a painkiller for diarrhea rather than going to the toilet. The natural correction is never allowed to happen until the bubble grows so large that the eventual collapse becomes catastrophic. This is a refusal to accept the system's inherent negativity—the very negativity that, in a properly functioning system, would discharge through contained failures. And if this negativity really can not be contained, then we shall reach tomorrow at a new synthesis, where the Absolute takes one step forward.

Consider the current Large Language Model (LLM) bubble. We can say that the system is in a state of profound misalignment. The core innovation around LLMs themselves has been largely stagnant for the past two to three years. What is hyperactive is the periphery: fine-tuning, agents, MCPs—all the operational scaffolding. Meanwhile, valuations remain sky-high even as the foundational technology hits diminishing returns. According to Hayek's own business cycle theory, this is a classic case of malinvestment. Capital was allocated to a specific sector based on a narrative of future productivity that is increasingly failing to materialize. Yet because billions of dollars have already been poured into the sector, the market is effectively lying to itself to maintain the illusion of growth. Every quarter, a CEO of a major AI firm appears on stage to declare that AGI is just around the corner, or has already been achieved. It is always the next model that will justify the current valuations. Hayek's theory of spontaneous order assumes that prices transmit real information. But in a bubble, the market is driven less by profit-seeking than by the fear of loss. Prices no longer convey actual value or utility-based information; they transmit the desperate need to keep the hype going. The system has entered a state of collective self-deception, where the very mechanism that is supposed to correct errors becomes the mechanism that perpetuates them.

The conventional macroeconomic response to such a situation is to blame "insufficient demand." The story goes: individuals aren't buying enough, so the state must step in to stimulate consumption. But this response strictly contradicts the cornerstone of liberalism that individuals possess sufficient knowledge and freedom to make their own decisions. If individuals are not buying, the liberal framework should first ask why. Is it because they have rationally assessed the available goods and found them wanting? Is it because their purchasing power has been eroded by structural factors? Or is it because the market, through its own distortions, is producing things that no one actually needs? To automatically default to "demand failure" is to treat individuals as passive recipients of macroeconomic stimuli rather than as autonomous agents. It also conveniently absolves the supply side—the producers, the investors, the financial engineers—from responsibility.

So, is Hayek really incompatible with Hegel? Only if we treat them as dogmas. When keeping almost only Hayek's economic methods and applying a Hegelian flavor to it, it sort of works.  The State, in this framework, is not the enemy of freedom but its condition, as a universal mediator that guarantees the baseline conditions under which individuals can actually exercise their freedom.
